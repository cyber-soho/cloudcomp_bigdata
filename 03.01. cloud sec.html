The Shared Responsibility Model

Understanding where the provider's job ends and yours begins.

This section explores the fundamental boundary in cloud computing. The provider secures the infrastructure "OF the Cloud", while you must secure what you put "IN the Cloud". Misunderstanding this line is the #1 cause of cloud breaches. Interact with the chart below to see how your responsibility shifts depending on the type of service you provision.

Responsibility Shift by Service Model

Key Insight: The higher up the stack, the less you manage — but you always own your data and access controls.

🏢 The "Flat" Analogy

Think of Cloud Security like renting an apartment in a large building.

  • Building Management (Provider): Secures the structure, main entrance, and fire escapes. If a burglar enters the main gate, it's their fault.
  • You, the Tenant (Customer): Responsible for locking your door, closing windows, and not storing hazards. If a burglar enters through your open window, it's your fault.

The CIA Triad

The three foundational pillars of security evaluation.

Every security decision should be evaluated through the CIA lens. All three pillars must hold simultaneously; sacrificing one creates unacceptable risk. Click on each pillar below to explore its specific goals, cloud controls, real-world breach examples, and how it maps to a standard data pipeline.

Confidentiality

"Who can see the data?"

Goal & Pipeline Stage

Prevent unauthorised disclosure of information.

Pipeline mapping: Data Ingestion

Cloud Controls

  • AES-256 encryption at rest
  • TLS 1.3 in transit
  • IAM policies & MFA
  • VPC isolation & KMS

Breach Example: An unauthorised nurse accesses a celebrity's medical records.

Integrity

"Has the data been altered?"

Goal & Pipeline Stage

Detect and prevent tampering or unauthorised modification.

Pipeline mapping: Processing (ETL)

Cloud Controls

  • SHA-256 checksums & hash digests
  • S3 versioning & immutable backups
  • CloudTrail logging
  • Database constraints & input validation

Breach Example: An attacker changes a patient's blood type in the database (data poisoning).

Availability

"Can we access the data when needed?"

Goal & Pipeline Stage

Maintain system and data uptime under all conditions.

Pipeline mapping: Storage (Warehouse)

Cloud Controls

  • Multi-AZ deployment & Auto-scaling
  • CDN distribution
  • DDoS protection (AWS Shield)
  • Health checks & disaster recovery

Breach Example: Ransomware locks doctors out of the system during a medical emergency.

Use the toggle to switch between these three critical defensive layers.

Identity and Access Management (The Gatekeeper)

IAM answers two questions: Authentication ("Who are you?") and Authorisation ("What can you do?"). The foundational rule is the Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP).

✅ Good Practice

  • s3:GetObject on *one specific bucket*
  • sagemaker:CreatePresignedUrl on *one notebook*

❌ Bad Practice

  • s3:* (all actions on all buckets)
  • Using the admin account "because it's easier"

Real-World Breach: Capital One (2019)

Impact: 106 million records exposed.

Cause: Overly permissive IAM role on a firewall. Attacker accessed metadata service, retrieved temp credentials, and accessed S3.

Lesson: A single misconfigured role compromises the entire account.

🛤️ Data In Transit

Data moving between systems. Standard: TLS 1.2 / 1.3.

  • OWASP Recommends: AES-256 (symmetric) + RSA ≥ 2048-bit (asymmetric).
  • Golden Rule: Never store encryption keys alongside the data they protect. A single breach exposes both.

Targeting dependencies (e.g., PyPI packages). Defence: Hash verification, lock files.
